# Asset Pricing Models and Factor Investing

## Source
Markowitz (1952), Sharpe (1964), Fama & French (1993, 2015), Carhart (1997)

## Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)
- Sharpe (1964): expected return = risk-free rate + beta × market risk premium
- Only systematic (market-wide) risk is priced; company-specific risk is diversified away
- Empirical failures: low-beta stocks earn too much; size and value effects unexplained

## Fama-French Three-Factor Model (1993)
Adds two factors beyond market beta:
- **SMB** (Small Minus Big): small-cap stocks tend to outperform large-cap
- **HML** (High Minus Low): value stocks (cheap relative to book value) outperform growth stocks

## Carhart Four-Factor Model (1997)
Adds momentum to the Fama-French model. Standard benchmark for evaluating mutual fund performance.

## Fama-French Five-Factor Model (2015)
Adds profitability (profitable firms outperform) and investment (conservative firms outperform aggressive ones).

## The Factor Zoo Problem
- Harvey, Liu & Zhu (2016): over 300 factors published that claim to predict returns
- Most are likely false discoveries from data mining — researchers test many variables and publish only the ones that happen to work historically
- Most factors fail to replicate out of sample

## Risk vs. Mispricing Debate
- **Risk-based view**: factors proxy for economic risk (e.g., value stocks are riskier in bad times); returns are fair compensation
- **Mispricing view (LSV 1994)**: value premium exists because investors extrapolate past growth rates too aggressively; behaviorally driven

## Anomaly Decay
- McLean & Pontiff (2016): after a factor is published, its returns tend to shrink
- Consistent with markets learning and correcting, or capital flows competing away the profit
- Average anomaly return decays ~26% post-publication

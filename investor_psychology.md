# Investor Psychology and Trading Behavior

## Source
Odean (1998, 1999), Barber & Odean (2000, 2001), Shefrin & Statman (1985)

## The Disposition Effect (Shefrin & Statman, 1985)
- Investors tend to sell winners too early and hold losers too long
- Directly predicted by Prospect Theory (loss aversion at reference point)
- Odean (1998) confirmed in large sample of individual investors
- Costly: sold winners outperform held losers by ~3.4% over next year

## Overconfidence in Trading
- Barber & Odean (2000): individual investors trade too much
- More trading = lower net returns after costs
- Men trade 45% more than women; single men trade most and earn least

## Home Bias
- Investors hold disproportionately large shares of domestic stocks
- Behavioral explanation: familiarity bias, overconfidence in local knowledge

## Herding Behavior
- Institutional herding: funds tend to buy/sell the same stocks together
- Informational cascades: investors discard private information and follow the crowd
- Can destabilize prices and amplify momentum

## Noise Trader Risk
- DeLong et al. (1990): irrational investors can push prices away from fundamentals
- Rational traders face risk that sentiment worsens before reverting
- Explains why smart money doesn't always correct mispricings quickly

## Investor Sentiment
- Baker & Wurgler (2006): aggregate investor sentiment predicts returns
- High sentiment predicts lower subsequent returns for speculative stocks
- Sentiment proxies: IPO volume, closed-end fund discounts, put-call ratios, surveys

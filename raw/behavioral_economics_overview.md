# Behavioral Economics Overview

## Source
Thaler & Sunstein "Nudge", Kahneman "Thinking Fast and Slow", survey articles

## Definition
Behavioral economics integrates insights from psychology into economic models. Classical economics assumes rational agents who maximize expected utility. Behavioral economics documents systematic deviations from this benchmark.

## Core Founders
- **Daniel Kahneman & Amos Tversky** – Prospect Theory (1979), Heuristics and Biases program
- **Richard Thaler** – Mental accounting, nudge theory, winner's curse
- **Robert Shiller** – Irrational exuberance, narrative economics
- **George Akerlof** – Market for lemons, identity economics

## Key Theoretical Frameworks

### Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979)
- People evaluate outcomes as gains/losses relative to a reference point
- Loss aversion: losses loom larger than equivalent gains (~2x weight)
- Diminishing sensitivity: marginal value decreases away from reference point
- Probability weighting: overweight small probabilities, underweight large ones

### Dual Process Theory (System 1 / System 2)
- System 1: fast, automatic, intuitive — prone to biases
- System 2: slow, deliberate, analytical — effortful but accurate
- Most economic decisions rely on System 1

### Mental Accounting (Thaler)
- People categorize money into separate mental accounts
- Violates fungibility of money
- Explains: reluctance to sell losing stocks, house money effect, sunk cost fallacy

## Key Biases
- **Overconfidence**: people overestimate accuracy of their beliefs
- **Anchoring**: judgments pulled toward arbitrary reference points
- **Availability heuristic**: judge probability by ease of recall
- **Status quo bias**: prefer current state over alternatives
- **Herding**: follow the crowd rather than independent analysis

## Policy Applications
- Nudge theory: design choice architectures that steer behavior
- Automatic enrollment in 401(k) plans dramatically increases savings rates
- Default rules matter enormously for outcomes

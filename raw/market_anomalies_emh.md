# Market Anomalies and the Efficient Market Hypothesis

## Source
Fama (1970, 1991), Shiller (1981), DeBondt & Thaler (1985), Jegadeesh & Titman (1993)

## Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)
Fama (1970): asset prices fully reflect all available information.
- **Weak form**: prices reflect all past trading data
- **Semi-strong form**: prices reflect all public information
- **Strong form**: prices reflect all public AND private information

## Documented Anomalies

### Return Predictability
1. **Momentum (Jegadeesh & Titman, 1993)** – stocks with high recent returns continue outperforming for the next 3–12 months. Behavioral explanation: investor underreaction and herding.
2. **Reversal / Mean Reversion (DeBondt & Thaler, 1985)** – long-term losers outperform long-term winners over 3–5 year horizons. Consistent with overreaction to news.
3. **Value Premium (Fama & French)** – high book-to-market stocks outperform low book-to-market stocks. Debated: risk premium vs. mispricing.

### Calendar Anomalies
1. **January Effect**: small-cap stocks outperform in January (tax-loss selling in December)
2. **Monday Effect**: stocks average lower returns on Mondays

### Firm-Level Anomalies
1. **Size effect**: small-cap stocks outperform large-cap stocks (Banz 1981)
2. **Post-Earnings Announcement Drift (PEAD)**: prices continue drifting in direction of earnings surprise for weeks/months
3. **IPO underperformance**: newly public firms underperform over 3–5 years

## Rational vs. Behavioral Explanations
- **Rational camp (Fama)**: anomalies reflect unpriced risk; data mining bias inflates apparent predictability
- **Behavioral camp (Shiller, Thaler)**: anomalies reflect systematic investor mistakes that arbitrage cannot fully eliminate

## Limits to Arbitrage
Even if mispricing exists, it may persist because:
- Asset can become more mispriced before correcting
- Short-selling is costly and risky
- Rational traders face career risk if they bet against the crowd too early

## The Joint Hypothesis Problem
Testing market efficiency always requires a model of expected returns. A rejection of EMH could mean markets are inefficient OR the asset pricing model is wrong — you can never fully separate the two.
